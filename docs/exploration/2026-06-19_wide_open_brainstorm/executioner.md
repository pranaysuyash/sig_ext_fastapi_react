# Executioner

This is the Executioner pass for the `wide open brainstorm` room for Signature Extractor.

Blunt verdict: do not build this as a broad product.

The strongest honest case against it is that the market is already full of better, more familiar, more defensible tools for the same job-shaped gap:

- If the user needs to sign, they already have Preview, Acrobat, browser PDF tools, or DocuSign.
- If the user needs a reusable signature image, they already have screenshots, image editors, and file storage.
- If the user needs legal or compliance-grade signing, they want a workflow product with identity, audit, and policy, not a local utility.
- If the user needs document editing, they want a real PDF editor, not a signature-specific side quest.

That means the core pain is not missing. It is just not painful enough, often enough, to fund a standalone company or a big product surface.

## Why this should be abandoned

### Wrong timing
The category is mature and crowded. Users already know the shape of the solution, and they already have enough defaults to get by. No one is sitting around waiting for a new signature extractor unless they are already trapped inside a very specific workflow.

### Wrong audience
The obvious audience is broad enough to be meaningless: anyone who signs documents. That is not an audience. Real buyers are narrower: legal ops, admin-heavy teams, founders, support staff, and occasional solo users. Most of them will not adopt a new tool unless the pain is constant and the trust bar is high.

### Already solved
The market has solved the visible part of the problem. The remaining gap is edge-case polish, not category invention. That is a bad place to build from because polish does not create a moat.

### Wrong founder
This is not the kind of problem that rewards a clever local app alone. If the founder does not already own a distribution channel into document-heavy workflows, the product becomes a feature waiting for a host. If the unfair advantage is computer vision, signature extraction is too thin to carry it.

### Market won't pay
This is utility software with intermittent usage. Intermittent pain is terrible pricing territory. Users will tolerate friction before they pay meaningfully for convenience, especially when the workaround is already on their machine.

### Regulatory trap
The minute the story shifts from "extract a signature" to "manage signing," you walk into legal-risk territory: identity, consent, evidentiary chain, retention, auditability, jurisdiction, and claims about authenticity. A local-first product makes the trust story harder, not easier, unless it is deliberately narrow. That is a trap, not a moat.

### Premise collapse
The premise sounds bigger than it is. "Signature operations workspace" is a nice phrase, but most of the actual value collapses into one of two tiny jobs:

1. crop or clean a signature image,
2. place it somewhere and export.

That is not a platform. That is a utility. Utilities can be loved. They usually cannot be venture-scale unless they are the gateway to a much bigger workflow.

## The kill case in one sentence

This is a convenience feature with compliance-adjacent risk masquerading as a product thesis.

## The only three salvageable concepts

These are the only pieces worth keeping if someone insists on extracting value before killing the rest. They are not enough to justify the full build.

### 1. `Signature Vault`
A tiny local store for the last good signature asset.

Why it is the least bad idea:
- it reduces repeat work,
- it matches a real recurring need,
- it stays narrowly scoped.

Why it still does not save the product:
- a vault alone is not a business,
- it is easy to copy,
- it does not create habit or switching cost.

### 2. `Proof Before Export`
A visible preflight check before a PDF leaves the app.

Why it is the least bad idea:
- it catches obvious mistakes,
- it adds trust,
- it aligns with the real risk point.

Why it still does not save the product:
- every PDF tool can add a review step,
- it is a safeguard, not a category,
- it does not solve demand.

### 3. `Quiet Reentry`
Let the user reopen a prior signature job and land exactly where they left off.

Why it is the least bad idea:
- it helps repeated work,
- it makes the tool feel thoughtful,
- it is the only path toward actual retention.

Why it still does not save the product:
- retention on a rare task is still weak,
- context replay is nice but not decisive,
- it is not enough to overcome distribution gravity.

## One non-obvious insight

The problem is not that people need signatures. The problem is that they need signatures too infrequently to build a strong habit around a new tool.

That is the real collapse point. Frequency is too low, urgency is too episodic, and default tools are too acceptable. Without repeated pain, there is no wedge.

## Final position

Kill the broad idea.

If anything survives, it should survive only as a narrow utility inside a larger document workflow, not as a standalone vision. The wide open brainstorm should not confuse "there are many possible features" with "there is a company here."
